Ask for a Raise (Without Threatening to Quit or Making It Weird)

You're underpaid and you know it. But asking for more money feels like defusing a bomb - one wrong move and your job explodes. This prompt helps you build an airtight case, pick the perfect timing, and negotiate like someone who knows their worth - without ultimatums or awkwardness.

The Prompt:

Why This Prompt Works:

Most people fail at raise negotiations because they make it about need instead of value, or they pick terrible timing. This prompt helps you approach it like a business case - with data, strategy, and multiple fallback positions so you win something even if you don't get everything.

Example Input:

ROLE: Marketing Manager, 2.5 years in position

CURRENT: $65k, last raise 18 months ago (3%)

TARGET: $78k (20% increase)

COMPANY: Doing well, just got Series B funding

PERFORMANCE: Increased leads 40%, managed product launch

MARKET: Similar roles paying $72-85k

BOSS: Good relationship, values data

TIMING: Annual review in 3 weeks

LEVERAGE: Recruited contacted me, only one who knows our CRM

CONCERN: They'll think I'm greedy

Raise Negotiation Rules:

  • Thursday morning: Statistically best time to ask
  • Never on Monday: Everyone hates Monday
  • After a win: Strike when your value is obvious
  • Numbers > Feelings: Data beats "I deserve"
  • Ask for 20%: To get 10-15%
  • Practice out loud: 5 times minimum
  • Get it in writing: Or it didn't happen
  • Stay employed: Don't threaten to quit unless you will

Build Your Case With:

  • Revenue generated: "Brought in $X in new business"
  • Money saved: "Cut costs by $Y through..."
  • Efficiency gains: "Reduced process time by Z%"
  • Problems solved: "Fixed issue that was costing..."
  • Team impact: "Trained X people, improved retention"
  • Scope creep: "Now doing Manager role without title"
  • Market rates: "Similar roles paying X% more"

Mistakes That Kill Raises:

  • Making it about your bills/rent/life costs
  • Comparing to specific coworkers
  • Threatening to quit (unless you will)
  • Getting emotional or angry
  • Accepting "we'll review later" without date
  • Not having a specific number
  • Apologizing for asking
  • Taking the first offer

Companies expect you to negotiate - they budgeted for it. The only thing standing between you and more money is this conversation. Use this prompt to make your case bulletproof and your confidence unshakeable. You've earned it, now go get it.
